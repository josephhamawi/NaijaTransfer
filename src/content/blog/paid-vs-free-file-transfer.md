Most free file-transfer tiers are loss leaders. They exist to get you into the product, not to be used for real work. That's a perfectly honest deal, but it means the free tier is designed to hit a limit before you can do much serious with it. Knowing which limit hurts first tells you whether to upgrade.

I ran our own pipeline on a free tier for about eight weeks. Cost of upgrading: NGN 4,500 a month. Cost of the free tier in re-uploads, lost links, and "sorry, can you resend" messages: about four billable hours a month. At any freelance rate above NGN 1,200 an hour, the math stops being close.

## The four features that matter

Not every paid feature is worth paying for. The ones that move the economics are narrower than the pricing page suggests.

### 1. Size cap

Free tiers typically cap at 2 GB per transfer. If your average deliverable is under 1 GB, this doesn't matter. If it's a photo gallery, a podcast ep, or any video, you're hitting the wall. Every transfer you have to split into two transfers is roughly 3x the clicks on both ends.

### 2. Expiry control

The default "7-day link" model is what kills more client relationships than any other limit. Clients don't always open email on your schedule. I sent a deliverable to a brand manager on a Wednesday; she was on maternity leave, opened it two Wednesdays later, file gone. I re-uploaded 14 GB on hotel wifi at a wedding. Never again. Paid tiers let you set 14, 30, or 90 days, or kill a link manually on demand.

### 3. Password protection and download limits

Free tiers often don't include either. For client work involving unreleased material (music, film, brand launches), you want both. Password stops the link being forwarded. Download limit stops someone grabbing the file twice from two different devices. We dig into what a password actually does (and doesn't) over in a separate post on the security side.

### 4. Download tracking

Knowing whether the client actually downloaded the file saves you the "did you get it?" email. That's five minutes you don't spend. Over a month of client work, it's a billable hour back.

## The break-even

Rough math. If you're sending more than three transfers a week, at least one of which is over 2 GB, and you're billing clients (not doing personal backup), the paid tier pays for itself in the first week. The NGN 4,500 to 9,000 range where most Nigerian-priced transfer tools sit is less than one hour of freelance work. You need exactly one "wait, the link died" incident a month to justify it.

If you're sending fewer than three a month and nothing is time-sensitive, free is fine. Just don't use it for billable client work where the relationship matters.

## What you're paying for

The pricing page lists features. What you're buying is different: predictable delivery. The paid tier is insurance against the specific failure modes that cost you more to recover from than the subscription costs. See [our pricing](/pricing) for the tier breakdown.

This matches the general finding from [Nairametrics reporting on SaaS adoption among Nigerian SMBs](https://nairametrics.com/), which shows that tools solving "embarrassment in front of clients" get retained far longer than tools promising abstract efficiency gains. A dead link at the wrong moment is a brand event, not a technical one.

## The scenarios where free is genuinely fine

Passing a contract PDF to your accountant: use email or free tier, doesn't matter.

Sharing a podcast draft with a co-host who's going to re-download it an hour later: free is fine.

Sending a 30 MB logo to a printer: don't even need a transfer tool, just email it.

Anything creative, anything client-facing, anything above 1 GB: upgrade. The cost is roughly the same as two coffees at a Lagos cafe per month. The pain it saves is real.

For the specific cases where free won't even start, like [uploading a 10 GB file on Nigerian broadband](/blog/uploading-10gb-file-nigerian-broadband), the free tier won't finish the job regardless of budget. Pick the tier that matches what you actually do.
