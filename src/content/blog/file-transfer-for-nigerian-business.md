Business-grade file transfer isn't a different technology from consumer file transfer. It's the same transfer layer plus three things consumer tools don't bundle: audit logs, user management, and contractual terms that protect the business using it. Once you see it that way, the question of "when to upgrade to business" has a cleaner answer.

A studio sending one or two deliverables a week to end clients can get by on a consumer paid tier indefinitely. An agency sending twenty deliverables a week to multiple clients across a team of five? You're already losing time to workflow friction that a business plan would fix. The math breaks at team size, not transfer volume.

## What changes when you go from consumer to business

### User management

On a consumer plan, everyone shares a login or has their own personal account. Shared logins die when someone leaves. Individual accounts mean you have no view of what's being sent on behalf of the business. Business plans give you a team workspace: add a user, they upload under the team, revoke them on their last day, everything they sent stays accessible.

### Audit logs

Who uploaded which file, when, to whom, with what expiry. A consumer plan has none of this. A business plan has all of it. For NDPR compliance, for insurance disputes, and for the inevitable "I never got that file" call, this is the feature that earns its keep.

### DPA and SCCs

Data Processing Agreement and Standard Contractual Clauses. Your clients with European customers will start asking for these once you grow past a certain size. Consumer plans don't include them. Business plans do. If you ever have to answer an RFP from a regulated client, this is page one.

### SSO and access control

Single sign-on, role-based access, IP restrictions. Matters most for law firms, accounting firms, and anyone with clerical staff who shouldn't see every file the partners send.

## Speed matters differently for business

For an individual creator, a 20-minute vs 40-minute upload is an annoyance. For a business with a producer coordinating delivery for four projects simultaneously, the aggregate time cost is significant. One hour a day lost to slow uploads across a 5-person team is 100 hours a month. That's a part-time salary.

Business tools with Lagos-resident origin servers meaningfully change this math. The CDN choice is the single biggest variable. Cloudflare, for example, publishes real-time latency data on their [Cloudflare Radar](https://radar.cloudflare.com/) dashboard, and the gap between a Nigerian edge and a European edge is visible in every Nigerian workday.

## The price point where business makes sense

Rough heuristic for Nigerian agencies and studios: business-tier file transfer pays for itself when you have at least two full-time people who send client deliverables, plus a repeat client who has asked you about compliance once.

At NGN 25,000 to 40,000 a month for a small-team business plan (the range [our business plan](/business) falls into), you're paying roughly one billable hour per week per team member to solve the problems we just listed. That's cheap. The moment one audit request lands and you can answer it in five minutes instead of five days, the plan has paid for itself twice over.

## A specific scenario

Imagine a Lagos-based content studio with a producer, two editors, and a motion designer. They deliver roughly fifteen projects a month to eight clients. They've been on a consumer paid tier for three years, shared login, no audit trail. One client (a fintech) asks for evidence that a particular deliverable was sent on time. They can't produce it: the email is there, the link died 60 days ago, no server-side record.

Upgrade to business, same team, NGN 30,000 a month. The producer adds all four team members as users, each uploads under their own name, audit logs kept indefinitely on the paid tier, download receipts kept per transfer. Next time the fintech asks, it's a 3-minute answer, including a screenshot. That's the kind of operational upgrade that keeps retainer clients.

## The law firm case

Law firms in Lagos are the most underserved by consumer transfer tools. They deal with large PDF evidence bundles, they need confidentiality, and they deal with courts that don't care about technology. For them, the critical features are password protection, short expiry, download notifications, and a clean paper trail showing exactly when opposing counsel accessed a file. A business tier covers all of this. A consumer tier covers none of it properly.

I've seen partners at mid-sized Nigerian firms still using email attachments for 800 MB evidence bundles in 2026. That's two problems: the file usually fails to send, and the firm has no record of delivery. A business-tier transfer tool at NGN 35,000 a month fixes both. It's a rounding error against the billing rates.

## What the business tier should not do

Oversell. Be wary of vendors pitching "enterprise features" that are really consumer features with enterprise badges. The test is simple: does the plan give you user management, audit logs, a DPA, and real customer support? If yes, it's a business plan. If it's mostly marketing language about "empowering teams," keep looking.
