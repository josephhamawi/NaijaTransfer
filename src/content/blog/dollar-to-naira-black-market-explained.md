If you have ever paid for school fees abroad, bought online from a foreign site, or sent a sibling some pocket money to London, you have already used the parallel-market rate. The "black market" name sticks because the headlines like it, but the rate itself is not shadowy. It is the price at which licensed Bureau de Change operators and individuals exchange foreign currency outside the official Nigerian Foreign Exchange Market. It moves throughout the day. It clears at whatever the dollar is worth to people right now, not what the Central Bank wishes it was worth.

This is a guide to reading that rate the way a Lagos importer reads it: as a number that already factors in scarcity, demand, location, and timing. ## Two markets, one naira

Nigeria has run two effective FX markets for decades. The official market is where banks settle imports, oil receipts, and corporate payments at a rate the Central Bank publishes. The parallel market is where the rest of demand clears — travel allowances, online subscriptions, personal remittances, school fees, anything the official window cannot or will not absorb fast enough.

Before President Tinubu's June 2023 decision to let the naira float, the gap between the two markets sometimes exceeded 60 percent. A dollar that cost ₦465 at the CBN window cost over ₦750 from a Lagos BDC. After the float and the October 2023 unification under a "willing buyer, willing seller" framework, the spread collapsed sharply. As of May 2026 it sits between 2 and 6 percent on most days. Not zero, but no longer absurd.

The reason it is not zero comes down to plumbing. The parallel market is cash-heavy and clears in minutes. The official market goes through banks, takes longer, and rations dollars on a weekly basis. A small premium for speed and certainty is a permanent feature, not a bug.

## Why the rate is not a single number

When you see "USD/NGN ₦1,395" quoted on a website, that is the buy rate — what the BDC will charge you for one dollar. The sell rate is what they will pay you when you bring dollars in. The gap between the two is the dealer's margin and is usually ₦5 to ₦20 per dollar in Lagos, sometimes more in smaller cities.

Both numbers move several times a day. Demand spikes when school terms start abroad, when crude oil prices fall (because dollar inflows shrink), when there is regulatory news, or when the naira sells off in any currency forum. A quote you saw at 9 am in Allen, Ikeja, may not match what the same dealer offers at 3 pm.

There are also geographic spreads. Lagos is the most liquid market and has the tightest dealer margins. Abuja runs a few naira tighter than Lagos on most days. Kano, historically, has been the cheapest dollar in the country — its position as a trading hub for the Sahel keeps cash dollars circulating. Port Harcourt sits in the middle.

## How to read today's number

If you are about to spend foreign currency in size — paying tuition, buying a car part from Alibaba, settling a freelance invoice — three numbers matter, in this order.

First, the **parallel-market sell rate**. That is what the BDC will pay you for $1 if you want to convert dollars to naira. It is the floor of what your foreign currency is worth to you in cash today.

Second, the **parallel-market buy rate**. That is what they will charge you to acquire $1 in cash. If you need to send money out, this is the cost of the dollars before any wire fees.

Third, the **official NFEM rate**. This is the number your bank will use if you do an outbound wire transfer. It is usually a few naira tighter than the parallel rate. For amounts above $10,000, the bank route often beats the cash route once you factor in handling and security.

The premium between parallel and official tells you whether the dollar is in excess supply or short supply this week. A widening premium (above 5 percent) usually means dollars are scarce — importers are scrambling, oil receipts have lagged, or there has been a regulatory shock. A narrowing premium (below 3 percent) means the official window is meeting demand and the parallel market is loosely connected to it.

## Why the rates we publish are indicative, not a quote

Every site that publishes parallel-market rates — including this one — sources them from public submissions, BDC operator quotes, and aggregators. They are accurate to within ₦5 to ₦20 per dollar on a typical day, which is good enough for context but not a guarantee for any specific transaction.

If you are about to transact, three rules:

1. Confirm the rate with the actual dealer or bank. Cash counters move minute to minute.
2. Check that the BDC is registered with [ABCON](https://abcon.org.ng). Unregistered street operators are not legal counterparties and you have no recourse if a deal goes wrong.
3. For amounts above ₦5,000,000, do not carry cash across the city. Use a bank transfer or a regulated remittance service. The premium you save on the parallel rate is not worth the risk.

## What changed in 2026

The Central Bank re-admitted licensed BDCs to the official Nigerian Foreign Exchange Market on 11 February 2026, with a $150,000-per-week purchase cap per dealer. The practical effect was to narrow the spread further. BDCs can now buy dollars at the official rate during weekly auctions and resell them at the market-clearing parallel rate, capturing the spread legally. As of early May 2026, the average parallel premium is about 2 percent — the tightest since the float.

This does not mean the parallel market is going away. As long as the official window rations dollars, individual demand from travellers and importers will keep the parallel rate alive. But the days of headline-grabbing 60 percent spreads are, for now, behind us.
